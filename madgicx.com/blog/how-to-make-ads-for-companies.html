How to Make Ads for Companies: The Complete Framework

Before diving into the 15 steps, it's essential to understand that learning how to make ads for companies requires a systematic approach. Whether you're creating ads for your own business or developing advertising strategies for clients, the fundamentals remain consistent: understand your audience, craft compelling messages, and optimize for measurable results.

1. Define Your Advertising Goals and KPIs

Before you spend a single dollar on ads, you need to know exactly what success looks like for your business. I can't tell you how many e-commerce owners I've met who were "doing advertising" but had no clue if it was actually working.

Your advertising goals should be SMART: Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, and Time-bound. Here are the most common goals for e-commerce businesses:

Revenue-Based Goals:

  • Increase monthly revenue by 30% within 90 days
  • Generate $50,000 in sales from advertising this quarter
  • Achieve a 4:1 return on ad spend (ROAS) consistently

Growth-Based Goals:

  • Acquire 1,000 new customers per month
  • Increase email list by 5,000 subscribers
  • Expand into 3 new geographic markets

Brand Awareness Goals:

  • Reach 100,000 people in your target demographic monthly
  • Increase brand search volume by 25%
  • Build a social media following of 10,000 engaged followers

The key is choosing 2-3 primary goals and tracking the right KPIs. For most e-commerce businesses, I recommend focusing on:

  • Cost Per Acquisition (CPA): How much you pay to get a new customer
  • Return on Ad Spend (ROAS): Revenue generated for every dollar spent
  • Customer Lifetime Value (CLV): Total revenue from a customer over time
  • Conversion Rate: Percentage of ad clicks that become purchases
Pro Tip: Use Madgicx's profit tracking to automatically calculate true ROAS including all costs – shipping, product costs, and platform fees. Most businesses are shocked to discover their "profitable" campaigns are actually losing money when all costs are included.

2. Research and Define Your Target Audience

Here's a truth bomb: if you're trying to sell to everyone, you're selling to no one. The most successful ads speak directly to a specific person with specific problems, dreams, and desires.

Your target audience research should cover three key areas:

Demographics (The Basics):

  • Age, gender, location, income level
  • Education, job title, industry
  • Family status, homeownership

Psychographics (The Good Stuff):

  • Values, interests, hobbies
  • Pain points and challenges
  • Goals and aspirations
  • Shopping behavior and preferences

Behavioral Data (The Gold Mine):

  • Website browsing patterns
  • Purchase history and frequency
  • Social media engagement
  • Email interaction rates

Start by analyzing your existing customers. Look at your best buyers – the ones with highest order values and repeat purchases. What do they have in common?

Use tools like Google Analytics, Facebook Audience Insights, and customer surveys to gather this data.

Create detailed buyer personas for your top 2-3 customer segments. Give them names, faces, and backstories. When you're writing ad copy later, you'll write to "Sarah, the busy mom who values organic products" instead of "women aged 25-45."

Pro Tip: Leverage AI audience insights to discover profitable customer segments you might have missed. Platforms like Facebook's Advantage+ audiences use machine learning to find people similar to your best customers, often uncovering segments you never considered.

3. Analyze Your Competition

Your competitors are running a free masterclass in advertising – you just need to know how to watch. Competitive analysis isn't about copying (that's boring and ineffective), it's about finding gaps and opportunities.

Here's your competitive research checklist:

Ad Creative Analysis:

  • What types of images/videos are they using?
  • What's their brand aesthetic and messaging tone?
  • How often do they refresh their creative?
  • What offers and promotions do they run?

Platform Strategy:

  • Which platforms are they most active on?
  • What's their posting frequency and engagement rates?
  • Are they running video ads, carousel ads, or static images?

Targeting and Positioning:

  • Who are they targeting (look at their page followers)?
  • What pain points do they address in their copy?
  • How do they position against other competitors?
  • What unique selling propositions do they emphasize?

Use tools like Facebook Ad Library (free), SEMrush, or SpyFu to research competitor ads. Save screenshots of ads that catch your attention and analyze what makes them effective.

The goal isn't to copy but to identify:

  • Gaps in their messaging you can fill
  • Audiences they're not targeting
  • Creative styles that aren't being used
  • Problems they're not solving

4. Set Your Advertising Budget

Ah, the million-dollar question: "How much should I spend on ads?" The answer depends on your business goals, profit margins, and growth stage, but here's a framework that works for most e-commerce businesses.

The 50/30/20 Rule for Ad Budgets:

  • 50% on your best-performing platform (usually Facebook/Instagram for e-commerce)
  • 30% on your second-best platform (often Google Ads)
  • 20% for testing new platforms and strategies

Budget Allocation by Business Stage:

Startup/Testing Phase ($500-2,000/month):

  • Start with $20-50/day on one platform
  • Focus on learning and data collection
  • Prioritize conversion tracking setup

Growth Phase ($2,000-10,000/month):

  • Scale successful campaigns gradually
  • Add second platform when first is profitable
  • Invest in creative production and testing

Scale Phase ($10,000+/month):

  • Expand to multiple platforms
  • Increase creative production volume
  • Consider automation tools for optimization

The Golden Rule: Never spend more than 30% of your profit margin on advertising. If your profit margin is 40%, your maximum ad spend should be 12% of revenue to maintain healthy cash flow.

Remember, Google Ads average ROI ranges from 200% to 800% when managed properly, making advertising one of the highest-return investments for growing businesses.

5. Choose Your Advertising Platforms

Not all advertising platforms are created equal, and trying to be everywhere at once is a recipe for mediocre results. Here's how to choose the right platforms for your business:

Facebook & Instagram (Meta):

  • Best for: Visual products, B2C, audiences aged 25-65
  • Strengths: Advanced targeting, visual creative options, detailed analytics
  • Budget recommendation: Start with $30-50/day
  • Success tip: Focus on video content and user-generated content

Google Ads:

  • Best for: High-intent searches, B2B, competitive markets
  • Strengths: Captures demand when people are actively searching
  • Budget recommendation: Start with $20-40/day
  • Success tip: Begin with exact match keywords and expand gradually

TikTok Ads:

  • Best for: Younger audiences (16-34), trendy products, entertainment
  • Strengths: High engagement rates, viral potential, creative freedom
  • Budget recommendation: Start with $20/day minimum
  • Success tip: Create native-feeling content that doesn't look like ads

LinkedIn Ads:

  • Best for: B2B products, professional services, high-ticket items
  • Strengths: Professional targeting, decision-maker reach
  • Budget recommendation: Start with $50/day (higher minimum costs)
  • Success tip: Focus on thought leadership and value-driven content

YouTube Ads:

  • Best for: Educational products, longer sales cycles, brand building
  • Strengths: Video storytelling, massive reach, Google integration
  • Budget recommendation: Start with $25/day
  • Success tip: Create compelling first 5 seconds to avoid skips

My recommendation: Start with Facebook/Instagram if you're B2C or Google Ads if you're B2B. Master one platform before expanding to others. It's better to be excellent on one platform than mediocre on five.

6. Develop Your Brand Messaging

Your brand messaging is the foundation of every ad you'll create. It's not just what you say – it's how you make people feel about your brand and why they should choose you over the competition.

The Brand Messaging Framework:

1. Brand Promise:

What transformation do you provide? Complete this sentence: "We help [target audience] achieve [desired outcome] by [unique method]."

Example: "We help busy professionals achieve better sleep by providing science-backed mattresses designed for optimal rest."

2. Unique Value Proposition:

What makes you different? Focus on benefits, not features.

Instead of: "Our mattresses have memory foam and cooling gel"

Say: "Fall asleep faster and wake up refreshed with our temperature-regulating sleep system"

3. Brand Personality:

How do you want to sound? Choose 3-4 personality traits:

  • Professional vs. Casual
  • Serious vs. Playful 
  • Traditional vs. Innovative
  • Authoritative vs. Friendly

4. Proof Points:

What evidence supports your claims?

  • Customer testimonials and reviews
  • Scientific studies or certifications
  • Awards and recognition
  • Usage statistics or case studies

5. Emotional Triggers:

What emotions drive your customers' decisions?

  • Fear of missing out (FOMO)
  • Desire for status or belonging
  • Need for security or comfort
  • Aspiration for improvement

Your messaging should be consistent across all platforms while adapting to each platform's unique culture. A LinkedIn ad might be more professional, while a TikTok ad can be playful and casual.

7. Create Compelling Ad Creative

Now we're getting to the fun part – creating ads that actually stop the scroll and make people want to buy. Great advertising design combines psychology, aesthetics, and strategic thinking.

The Creative Hierarchy:

1. Hook (First 3 Seconds):

Your hook needs to grab attention immediately. Use:

  • Bold, contrasting colors
  • Surprising statements or questions
  • Movement or animation
  • Faces showing strong emotions

2. Value Proposition (Seconds 3-8):

Clearly communicate your main benefit:

  • Show the product in use
  • Highlight the transformation
  • Display social proof
  • Demonstrate unique features

3. Call-to-Action (Final seconds):

Tell people exactly what to do next:

  • Use action words (Shop, Discover, Learn)
  • Create urgency when appropriate
  • Make the next step obvious

Creative Formats That Convert:

Static Images:

  • Product shots with lifestyle context
  • Before/after transformations
  • Customer testimonials with photos
  • Infographics with key benefits

Video Ads:

Research shows that video ads outperform other formats by 120%, making them essential for competitive markets.

  • Product demonstrations
  • Customer success stories
  • Behind-the-scenes content
  • Educational tutorials

Carousel Ads:

  • Multiple product views
  • Step-by-step processes
  • Feature comparisons
  • Customer journey stories

User-Generated Content:

  • Customer photos and videos
  • Reviews and testimonials
  • Unboxing experiences
  • Social media mentions

8. Write High-Converting Ad Copy

Great ad copy doesn't just describe your product – it speaks directly to your customer's desires, fears, and motivations. Here's how to write copy that converts browsers into buyers.

The AIDA Framework:

Attention: Grab them with a compelling headline

Interest: Build interest with benefits and social proof 

Desire: Create desire by addressing pain points and aspirations

Action: Drive action with a clear, compelling CTA

Headline Formulas That Work:

Problem/Solution:

"Tired of [problem]? Here's how [solution] changes everything."

Benefit-Driven:

"Get [desired outcome] in [timeframe] with [product/service]."

Social Proof:

"Join [number] of [target audience] who [achieved result]."

Curiosity Gap:

"The [adjective] [thing] that [surprising result]."

Copy Psychology Principles:

1. Lead with Benefits, Support with Features:

Instead of: "Our software has advanced analytics"

Write: "See exactly which marketing campaigns drive the most sales (with advanced analytics that break down every conversion)"

2. Use Social Proof:

  • "Trusted by 10,000+ businesses"
  • "4.8-star rating from verified customers"
  • "As seen in Forbes, TechCrunch, and Entrepreneur"

3. Address Objections:

  • Price: "Starting at just $19/month"
  • Risk: "30-day money-back guarantee"
  • Complexity: "Set up in under 5 minutes"

4. Create Urgency (When Genuine):

  • "Limited time offer"
  • "Only 50 spots available"
  • "Sale ends midnight tonight"

Platform-Specific Copy Tips:

Facebook/Instagram:

  • Keep it conversational and personal
  • Use emojis sparingly for emphasis
  • Include questions to boost engagement

Google Ads:

  • Match search intent closely
  • Include keywords in headlines
  • Highlight unique selling points

LinkedIn:

  • Professional tone and language
  • Focus on business benefits
  • Include industry-specific terms

9. Set Up Tracking and Analytics

Here's where most businesses mess up: they launch ads without proper tracking, then wonder why they can't tell what's working. Proper tracking setup is non-negotiable for profitable advertising.

Essential Tracking Setup:

1. Platform Pixels:

  • Facebook Pixel for Meta ads
  • Google Analytics 4 for all traffic
  • Google Ads conversion tracking
  • Platform-specific pixels for other channels

2. Conversion Events:

  • Page views and time on site
  • Add to cart and checkout initiation
  • Purchases and revenue
  • Email signups and form submissions

3. UTM Parameters:

Track campaign performance with consistent UTM codes:

  • utm_source (facebook, google, tiktok)
  • utm_medium (cpc, social, email)
  • utmcampaign (springsale_2025)
  • utmcontent (videoad_v1)

iOS Tracking Challenges:

Apple's iOS updates have made tracking more challenging, but there are solutions:

Server-Side Tracking:

Implement first-party data collection that bypasses browser restrictions. This is where tools like Madgicx's Cloud Tracking become invaluable – they provide server-side tracking that improves data accuracy and helps platforms optimize better.

Conversion API:

Set up Facebook's Conversions API to send data directly from your server to Facebook, improving attribution accuracy.

First-Party Data:

Focus on collecting email addresses and phone numbers to build your own customer database.

Attribution Windows:

Understand different attribution models:

  • 1-day click: Conservative, shows immediate impact
  • 7-day click: Balanced view of campaign performance 
  • 28-day click: Full attribution window (pre-iOS changes)

Key Metrics to Track:

  • Click-through rate (CTR)
  • Cost per click (CPC)
  • Conversion rate
  • Cost per acquisition (CPA)
  • Return on ad spend (ROAS)
  • Customer lifetime value (CLV)

10. Launch Your First Campaign

The moment of truth has arrived – it's time to launch your first campaign. Here's your pre-launch checklist to ensure everything goes smoothly.

Pre-Launch Checklist:

Technical Setup:

✅ Tracking pixels installed and tested

✅ Conversion events firing correctly

✅ Landing pages mobile-optimized

✅ Payment processing working smoothly

✅ Customer service team briefed on campaign

Campaign Settings:

✅ Target audience properly defined

✅ Budget and bidding strategy set

✅ Ad creative uploaded and approved

✅ Campaign schedule configured

✅ UTM parameters added to all links

Creative Assets:

✅ Images/videos meet platform specifications

✅ Copy includes clear call-to-action

✅ Landing page matches ad messaging

✅ Multiple creative variations ready for testing

Launch Strategy:

Start Small and Scale:

Begin with 20-30% of your planned budget to test performance before scaling up. This approach lets you identify issues early and optimize before investing heavily.

Campaign Structure:

  • Create separate campaigns for different objectives
  • Use ad sets to test different audiences
  • Include 3-5 ad variations per ad set for testing

Bidding Strategy:

For beginners, start with automatic bidding to let the platform optimize. Once you have data, you can switch to manual bidding for more control.

Common Launch Mistakes to Avoid:

  • Changing settings too frequently (wait 3-7 days for data)
  • Setting budgets too low for meaningful testing
  • Using only one creative variation
  • Targeting audiences that are too broad or too narrow
  • Not having a clear conversion goal

11. Monitor and Optimize Performance

Launching your ads is just the beginning – the real improvements happen in optimization. Here's how to turn good campaigns into great ones through systematic monitoring and improvement.

Daily Monitoring (5-10 minutes):

  • Check overall spend vs. budget
  • Review cost per result trends
  • Identify any technical issues
  • Pause obviously underperforming ads

Weekly Deep Dive (30-60 minutes):

  • Analyze performance by audience segment
  • Review creative performance and refresh tired ads
  • Adjust budgets based on performance
  • Test new audiences or creative variations

Monthly Strategic Review (2-3 hours):

  • Calculate true ROI including all costs
  • Analyze customer lifetime value trends
  • Review competitive landscape changes
  • Plan next month's testing priorities

Key Optimization Strategies:

Creative Optimization:

  • Refresh creative when frequency exceeds 3-4
  • Test different hooks, offers, and CTAs
  • Analyze which creative elements drive best performance
  • Scale winning creative across multiple audiences

Audience Optimization:

  • Expand successful audiences gradually
  • Create lookalike audiences from best customers
  • Exclude converted customers from acquisition campaigns
  • Test interest-based vs. behavior-based targeting

Budget Optimization:

  • Shift budget toward best-performing campaigns
  • Increase budgets by 20-50% when scaling
  • Pause campaigns with CPA above target
  • Test different bidding strategies

A/B Testing Framework:

Test one element at a time:

  • Headlines and copy
  • Images and videos
  • Audiences and targeting
  • Landing pages and offers
  • Bidding strategies

Performance Benchmarks:

  • CTR: 1-2% for most industries
  • Conversion Rate: 2-5% for e-commerce
  • ROAS: 4:1 minimum for profitability
  • CPA: Should be less than 30% of customer lifetime value

12. Scale Successful Campaigns

Once you've found winning campaigns, it's time to scale – but scaling requires strategy, not just throwing more money at what's working.

Scaling Strategies:

Vertical Scaling (Budget Increases):

  • Increase budgets by 20-50% every 3-7 days
  • Monitor performance closely during scaling
  • Be prepared to reduce budgets if performance declines
  • Don't increase budgets more than 100% in a single day

Horizontal Scaling (Audience Expansion):

  • Create lookalike audiences from your best customers
  • Expand geographic targeting to new regions
  • Test broader interest categories
  • Duplicate successful campaigns with new audiences

Creative Scaling:

  • Produce more variations of winning creative
  • Test different formats (video, carousel, static)
  • Create seasonal or trending variations
  • Develop user-generated content campaigns

Platform Scaling:

  • Expand successful campaigns to new platforms
  • Adapt creative for platform-specific formats
  • Test different campaign objectives
  • Cross-promote top performers

Scaling Warning Signs:

  • CPA increasing significantly
  • Conversion rates declining
  • Audience overlap warnings
  • Creative fatigue (high frequency)

The Scaling Sweet Spot:

Most successful e-commerce businesses find their scaling sweet spot at:

  • 3-5x their initial test budget
  • 2-3 primary platforms
  • 5-10 active creative variations
  • Monthly creative refresh cycles

Advanced Scaling Techniques:

  • Campaign budget optimization (CBO)
  • Automated rules for budget adjustments
  • Dynamic product ads for e-commerce
  • Cross-platform retargeting sequences

14. Measure True ROI and Profitability

Most businesses think they're profitable when they're actually losing money. Here's how to calculate true ROI and ensure your advertising actually drives profit, not just revenue.

True ROI Calculation:

Revenue from Ads: $10,000

Minus Cost of Goods Sold: $4,000 (40%)

Minus Advertising Spend: $2,000

Minus Fulfillment Costs: $800 (shipping, packaging, processing)

Minus Platform Fees: $300 (payment processing, marketplace fees)

True Profit: $2,900

True ROI: 145% (not the 500% you thought!)

Key Metrics for Profitability:

Customer Lifetime Value (CLV):

Calculate the total revenue from a customer over their entire relationship with your business. This helps justify higher acquisition costs for customers who buy repeatedly.

Payback Period:

How long it takes to recover your customer acquisition cost through profit. Aim for 3-6 months for most e-commerce businesses.

Contribution Margin:

Revenue minus variable costs (COGS, shipping, payment processing). This is the money available to cover advertising and fixed costs.

Monthly Recurring Revenue (MRR):

For subscription businesses, track predictable monthly revenue to understand long-term value.

Advanced Profitability Analysis:

Cohort Analysis:

Track customer behavior by acquisition month to understand how advertising quality affects long-term value.

Attribution Modeling:

Understand how different touchpoints contribute to conversions. Email marketing, for example, typically delivers an ROI of $42 for every $1 spent, making it an excellent complement to paid advertising.

Incrementality Testing:

Run geo-holdout tests to measure the true incremental impact of your advertising beyond what would have happened organically.

Profitability Optimization:

  • Focus on high-CLV customer segments
  • Optimize for profit, not just ROAS
  • Include all costs in campaign optimization
  • Test pricing strategies alongside advertising

FAQ

What's the minimum budget needed to start advertising?

You can start testing with as little as $20-30 per day on most platforms. However, for meaningful data and optimization, I recommend starting with at least $500-1,000 per month.

This allows you to test multiple audiences and creative variations while gathering enough data to make informed decisions. Remember, it's better to start small and scale up than to blow your entire budget on untested campaigns.

How long does it take to see results from advertising?

Most platforms need 3-7 days to optimize your campaigns, but you should see initial data within 24-48 hours. For meaningful results and optimization insights, plan for 2-4 weeks of consistent spending.

E-commerce businesses typically see their best performance after 30-60 days of continuous optimization. Don't make major changes in the first week – let the algorithms learn.

Should I hire an agency or do it myself?

It depends on your budget, time, and complexity needs. DIY makes sense if you have time to learn and budgets under $10,000/month. Agencies are worth considering for budgets over $20,000/month or complex multi-platform strategies.

However, AI-powered tools like Madgicx offer a middle ground – giving you advanced optimization capabilities and insights while maintaining control and reducing costs.

How do I know if my ads are profitable?

Calculate your true profit by subtracting all costs from revenue: cost of goods sold, advertising spend, fulfillment costs, and platform fees. Your customer lifetime value should be at least 3x your customer acquisition cost for sustainable profitability.

Track metrics beyond ROAS – focus on contribution margin and payback period to understand real profitability.

What's the best advertising platform for beginners?

For e-commerce businesses, I recommend starting with Facebook and Instagram due to their visual nature, detailed targeting options, and comprehensive learning resources. For B2B companies, Google Ads often provides better initial results due to high purchase intent.

Master one platform before expanding to others – it's better to excel on one platform than to be mediocre on multiple platforms.
